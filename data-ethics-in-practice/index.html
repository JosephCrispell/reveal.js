Improving Cancer mutation panels

ACCURACY

One of the first projects I worked on with with a bioinformatics company in Germany as an intern.

I worked in a team that was analysing data for building cancer panels - identifying mutations in our genomes associated with cancers

What was critical in this work was accuracy - can we accurately identify mutations associated with particular cancers?

Image credit: Illumina

To identify mutations, the team compared sequencing data from people with and without cancers.

The aim was to develop a panel of mutations that could be used to identify individuals at risk of particular cancers.

Cattle, possums, and bTB in New Zealand

SAMPLING

Another project investigated bovine tuberculosis in cattle.

A key consideration in this project, although only clear at the end was sampling design.

Image credit: Wikimedia

Bovine tuberculosis is a problem in cattle populations worldwide.

It is a disease of the lungs. In many countries controlling bovine tuberculosis means culling thousands of cattle each year.

The bacteria that causes bovine tuberculosis infects cattle but also many other animals.

Image credit: Wikimedia

I joined a team in New Zealand wanting to understand what role possum populations were playing.

The brushtail possum in New Zealand is a pest species introduced because of its fur.

There are now 30 million possums in New Zealand and they are often infected with the bacteria causing bovine tuberculosis.
A team in New Zealand had sampled hundreds of infected cattle and wildlife from across New Zealand.

Unfortunately, to address the question of transmission we needed samples from cattle and wildlife living in the same area at the same time.

So we couldn't quantify the direction of transmission but we did find that possums and cattle were often infected with the same strain and strong evidence for transmission to and from cattle.

Cattle, badgers, and bTB in Woodchester Park

REPRESENTATIVENESS

Another opportunity to study the transmission of the bacteria causing bovine tuberculosis came up in England.

Here, it isn't possums that are implicated, it's badgers.

Also, in England it is a much bigger problem. Thousands of herds are infected!

What was critical with there analyses was representativeness, understand where our data came from and how representative our results were of other areas.
At least part of the problem is political!

Almost every week there is some controversy about the different control options in the UK.

There was loads of evidence that cattle and badgers were infected with similar strains of bacteria but none saying who was infecting whom?
A major difference to the previous work in New Zealand was the size of the sampling areas.

Instead of hundreds of samples from across an entire country, we had hundreds of samples from a tiny 10km squared area in the South West of England.

With these data we were able analyse the movements of cattle and badgers alongside the similarlity of their bacteria and found that the bacteria was being transmitted between the cattle and badgers and most frequently from badgers to cattle.

Importantly though, we sampled a tiny area in the South West of England. Our results while important couldn't tell us much about what was going on in the rest of the country.

Our sampling area was too small and similar projects in other areas would be needed.

Mapping HIV in Côte d'Ivoire

ANONYMITY

A project I'm currently working really highlights the importance of anonymity.

I've joined a team hoping to map HIV risk in adolescents in Cote d'Ivoire in West Africa.

Every five or so years, comprehensive national surveys are conducted to quantify HIV prevalence.

We want to see whether we can delve into these data to establish indicators of risk that was more readily available and more regularly updated to get more rapid signals of risk.

Data source: Data for good

However there are two important aspects we are considering while we are planning this project:

1. How can we protect those vulnerable groups we aim to identify? From stigma for example.
2. With highly sensitive data like HIV test results and socio-economic status, how can we protect our data subjects identities?

Image source: Demographic Household Surveys

Methods such as random displacement can be used to protect individuals by masking their locations.

Any method weighs the importance of anonmynity against the utility of the infromation in the data.

BE TRANSPARENT

We must be transparent in our project, clearly documenting our data, our coding, our analyses, results and our intepretation.

Image credit: The Turing Way

BE OPEN

We must be open. Not working in silos, but publicising our projects, joining the open-source community of sharing with and learning from others.

Image credit: The Turing Way

BE MORE THAN REPRODUCIBLE

It isn't enough to be reproducible, we must build tools that others can easily understand, use, and adapt.
